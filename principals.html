The Five Principals of Flag Design

Here are five really powerful design tips for flags. Source
Back

  1. Keep it Simple

    anybody can remember and draw it

    The thing with flags is that they are meant to be seen from a distance and overlooking a important object. They thus cannot be overly complex, since those complexities will make the flag from a distance seem blurry.
    A good rule of thumb is "less is more." If you have an overly complex flag, try to find the parts that seem important and highlight them in your flag.
    Also, if you are designing a flag, it's recommended to draw them on a 3 inch by 5 inch piece of paper, since that is how most people see them (from a distance).

    The Good

    Amsterdam

    The Flag is a good example of simplicity. It simplifies the Seal of Amsterdam (shown here) into its most distinguisable elements (Red and x's). As a pretty badass flag, it has a life of its own in patriotic merchandise and sports teams.

    The Ugly

    West Virginia

    The Flag of West Virginia is overly complex. Its seal is too ornate and small, also also has too many details to be understandable at a distance. It's nearly indistinguishable from a distance compared to other US state flags.

  2. Use Meaningful Symbolism

    what the flag is should relate to what it stands for

    Flags should representative of the stuff that they represent (duh). They cannot both be too simple or too complex. Each flag should always have a least one symbol that it can imporate into its design. That symbolism can be represented in small things, such as a specific shade of color, or in larger items, such as the flag's shape or imagry.
    Good flags ususally have 1, 2, or 3 interesting bits of symbolism and that are used coehesively.
    Good flags' symbolism is usually often stylized and made more abstract.

    The Good

    Ukraine

    The light blue and yellow in Ukraine's flag represent the sky over wheat fields. Both the color and direction of the stripes of the flag are important aspects to it. It's simple and symbolic of the country, one of whose main exports are wheat.

    The Ugly

    Libya

    1977-2011

    Even though the green color of the flag was chosen for it's Islamic symbolism, its monocolor design is too simple to represent one country and if the flag is in greyscale (as in newsprint), it means absolutely nothing. Kind of fitting for a dictatorship.

  3. Use 2-3 Basic Colors

    don't use too many different or clashing colors

    A flag should not have too many colors. Unless its object is its multicolorednes (as in the pride flag), having too many colors makes the flag confusing and sometimes even clashing with itself.
    A good rule of thumb is to have only 2 or 3 basic flag colors in a flag, and to only use those basic colors (red, blue, green, black, yellow, and white). Don't make to colors clash and also divide darks with lights and vice versa.
    A flag designer should be mindful of cost, too. Having an overly colored flag can make the flag too costly for most people to buy.

    The Good

    Iroquis Confederacy

    By using two colors and powerful symbolism (Hiawatha's belt and wampum shell beads), the flag og the Iroquis confederacy is both symbolic, complex, and simple.

    The Ugly

    Virginia

    By having 18 colors in its state flag, the Virginian state flag is both hard to distinguish and difficult to manuafacture.

  4. No Lettering or Seals

    writing and small symbols are a no-no

    Flags, above all, are meant to be symbolic. When one writes letters or dumps a seal on a flag, it's not just unimaginative and lazy but also hard to read at a distance, sew together, or even color. When a flag has to have lettering on it, its symbolism has failed.
    As a rule of thumb, never put any seal or lettering on a flag (there are some really cool exceptions though).

    The Good

    Côtes D'Amor

    France

    Instead of the logo style that used by a lot of French administrative districts, this one pleasantly suprises by having a symplistic and symbolic (representative of it's coastline) design that has no lettering or seals.

    The Ugly

    Tampa

    Florida

    This flag has many problems, including its messy design and tiny seal. No suprise that this flag was not designed by a artist but instead by an accountant

  5. Be Distinctive and Be Related

    no plagarism, but use similarities to show connections

    This can be, perhaps, the most difficult design trait of flags. People have been designing flags for a very long time, and it may seem that many of the best designs have already been taken. However, a flag's symbols, shapes, and colors can recall other flags' power and imagry.
    This rule of often seen most in former colonies or provinces, who have to pay homage to their rulers, but also have to forge their own meanings.

    The Good

    Acadia

    Canada

    This flag, flown by former French subjects, still has its flag in its background. However, this flag also has a star in a position of honor. This star is representative of St. Mary, their national symbol and patron saint of mariners.
